\begin{abstract}
We present CardShark-RL, a reinforcement learning system for heads-up
limit 5-Card Draw Poker that compares two opponent-adaptation strategies:
explicit modeling, where a one-hot archetype identifier is fed directly
into the agent's observation, and implicit modeling, where the agent works
from a rolling window of 10 behavioral statistics and never sees an
opponent label. Both agents are trained with MaskablePPO
\cite{raffin2021stable} in a custom PettingZoo \cite{terry2021pettingzoo}
environment, with Optuna-based hyperparameter search \cite{akiba2019optuna}
and hand-crafted reward shaping. Evaluated over 10,000 hands each against
three scripted archetypes---Calling Station, Maniac, and Rock---the
explicit agent (Model~A) averages $+180.65$ BB/100, while the implicit
agent (Model~B) averages $+166.50$ BB/100 ($p = 0.029$). The gap is real
but modest. More interesting: in a 1,500-hand non-stationary tournament
where the opponent switches archetype mid-session, Model~B ($+160.35$
BB/100) edges out an oracle-labeled explicit agent ($+156.28$ BB/100).
We analyze the behavioral mechanism through which
Model~B exploits opponent draw-count patterns without ever knowing who
it is playing.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Poker forces an agent to reason under hidden information and adapt to an
opponent whose strategy it cannot observe directly. The combination of
imperfect information, sequential decisions, and an adversarial opponent
makes it a useful benchmark, because the skills it demands transfer to real decision problems.

Systems like Libratus \cite{brown2018libratus}, DeepStack
\cite{moravcik2017deepstack}, and Pluribus \cite{brown2019superhuman}
have solved large variants of Texas Hold'em by minimizing exploitability
via CFR \cite{zinkevich2007regret}. They are robust against any opponent.
What they are not designed to do is actively exploit a weak one. An
equilibrium strategy leaves money on the table against predictable
opponents; exploitative play picks it up.

We built CardShark-RL to investigate exploitative RL in 5-Card Draw,
a variant with a clean information structure (each player draws once,
revealing something about their hand) and enough strategic depth to be
interesting. The central question is whether an agent can learn to profit
from opponent behavioral patterns using only indirect evidence. No
archetype label, just the history of what the opponent has done.

The system implements two agents. Model~A gets the opponent's archetype
fed to it as a one-hot vector; it knows exactly who it is playing.
Model~B gets a rolling window of 10 behavioral statistics and has to
figure the rest out. We run Optuna-based hyperparameter search with a
Rock-weighted objective (to avoid agents that just farm easy wins),
use reward shaping to push toward aggression, and run a behavioral
matrix analysis, which is Model~B's post-draw action
distribution conditioned on opponent draw count, to understand what
implicit information it actually learned to use.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Poker AI}

CFR-based systems have dominated competitive poker AI since Polaris
\cite{zinkevich2007regret}. Libratus \cite{brown2018libratus} and
DeepStack \cite{moravcik2017deepstack} both solved heads-up no-limit
Hold'em at a superhuman level in 2017 through different approaches
to endgame solving \cite{ganzfried2015endgame}. Pluribus
\cite{brown2019superhuman} extended this to six-player play. All of
these are equilibrium-seeking: they are hard to beat, but they do not
specifically target opponent weaknesses.

Our work sits in a different space. We train explicitly to exploit
fixed behavioral patterns, accepting that the resulting policy would
be vulnerable against an adversary that adapts in return.

\subsection{Action Masking}

In 5-Card Draw, the agent has 35 possible actions but only a small
subset are legal at any step. Huang and Onta\~{n}{\'o}n
\cite{huang2022closer} show that hard action masking---setting illegal
action log-probabilities to $-\infty$ before the softmax---is both
theoretically sound and empirically far better than penalty-based
alternatives when invalid actions are frequent. We use MaskablePPO
\cite{raffin2021stable} for exactly this reason.

\subsection{Opponent Modeling}

There is a large literature on opponent modeling in multi-agent RL,
ranging from Bayesian type inference to neural latent embeddings.
The rolling-statistics approach we use for Model~B is conceptually
similar to behavioral profiling in poker theory: track VPIP,
Pre-draw Raise frequency, Aggression Factor, and draw tendencies over
a window of recent hands, and let the policy condition on those
statistics rather than on an explicit type label.

\subsection{Reward Shaping and HPO}

Ng et al.~\cite{ng1999policy} established that potential-based reward
shaping preserves optimal policies. We use two auxiliary shaping terms
to guide the agent toward aggressive exploitation. Hyperparameter
search uses Optuna \cite{akiba2019optuna} with TPE sampling and
median pruning over a 15-dimensional space.

\section{Environment}
\label{sec:env}

\subsection{Game Rules}

CardShark-RL implements limit heads-up 5-Card Draw. Both players post
a 1-chip ante. Pre-draw betting uses 2-chip bets (up to 4 raises).
Each player discards and draws 0--5 cards. Post-draw betting uses
4-chip bets (up to 4 raises), followed by showdown. A player can win
before showdown by making the opponent fold.

\subsection{Environment Implementation}

The game runs as a PettingZoo AEC environment \cite{terry2021pettingzoo},
wrapped by a Gymnasium-compatible \cite{brockman2016openai} single-agent
interface (\texttt{DrawPokerGymEnv}) that auto-steps the opponent and
exposes a standard \texttt{(obs, reward, done, info)} tuple to the RL
agent (\texttt{player\_0}).

\subsection{Action Space}

The action space is \texttt{Discrete(35)}: Fold (0), Call/Check (1),
Raise/Bet (2), and 32 draw actions (3--34) encoding a 5-bit bitmask
over card positions. During betting phases only actions 0--2 are valid;
during draw phases only 3--34 are. Action masking enforces this at
every step.

\subsection{Observations}

Both models share 23 base features: 10 for card encoding (normalized
rank and suit per card), 2 for hand strength (category and evaluation
score), 3 for pot state (size, bet-to-call, pot odds), 3 phase
indicators, 1 for opponent draw count (or $-1$ if pre-draw), and 4
strategic features (position, own raise count, opponent aggression flag,
opponent pre-draw raise flag).

Model~A appends a 3-dimensional one-hot opponent archetype vector,
giving a 26-dimensional observation.

Model~B appends a 10-dimensional rolling statistics vector computed
over the last $W$ hands: fold frequencies pre- and post-draw, raise
frequency post-draw, average cards drawn, raise-then-stand-pat
correlation, fold-after-drawing-3 frequency, raise-after-standing-pat
frequency, VPIP, PFR, and Aggression Factor. Total: 33 dimensions.

\subsection{Hand Evaluator}

Cards are encoded as integers $0$--$51$ where $\text{rank} = c \bmod 13$
and $\text{suit} = \lfloor c / 13 \rfloor$. Hand strength is a scalar
$= \text{category} \times 10^6 + \text{tiebreaker}$, with the
tiebreaker using base-14 rank encoding. This gives a total ordering
across all nine hand categories.

\subsection{Opponent Archetypes}

We wrote three scripted opponents:

\textbf{CallingStation} never raises and never folds. It draws optimally
(keeps best group, discards kickers). A completely passive opponent.

\textbf{Maniac} raises 70\% of the time when it can, folds only 5\% of
the time, and chases flush/straight draws 60\% of the time by drawing
one card.

\textbf{Rock} plays a tight range: folds unpaired hands pre-draw to any
bet, calls with a pair below Jacks, raises with Jacks-or-better, and
stands pat on two-pair or better. It is the hardest to exploit because
it rarely puts chips in without a strong hand.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{MaskablePPO}

Both agents use MaskablePPO \cite{raffin2021stable}, a PPO variant that
applies action masking directly in the policy update. The clipped
objective is:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t \left[
  \min\!\left( r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t,\;
  \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t \right)
\right]
\label{eq:ppo}
\end{equation}

where $r_t(\theta) = \pi_\theta(a_t|s_t) / \pi_{\theta_\text{old}}
(a_t|s_t)$ and $\hat{A}_t$ is the GAE \cite{schulman2016high}:

\begin{equation}
\hat{A}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l},
\quad \delta_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)
\label{eq:gae}
\end{equation}

Illegal actions are masked by setting their log-probabilities to
$-\infty$ before the softmax.

\subsection{Network Architecture}

Both models use a separate actor and critic MLP with no shared parameters.
Model~B (implicit) uses four hidden layers of 256 neurons each, chosen
by HPO. Model~A (explicit) uses three layers of 256 neurons.

\subsection{Reward Function}

The base reward is net chip profit: $r = \text{pot\_won} -
\text{chips\_invested}$ for the winner, $r = -\text{chips\_invested}$
for the loser. Two shaping terms are added \cite{ng1999policy}: a fold
penalty $(-\alpha)$ when the agent folds in a spot where checking was
free (bet-to-call = 0), and a steal bonus $(+\beta)$ when the agent
raises and the opponent folds. The weights are tuned by Optuna; for
Model~B, $\alpha = 0.296$ and $\beta = 0.448$.

\subsection{Opponent Scheduling}

Model~A uses random scheduling, meaning a new archetype is drawn uniformly before
each episode. Since it sees the archetype label directly, it can condition
immediately.

Model~B uses a hybrid schedule. The first $H$ episodes use block
scheduling: opponents cycle in blocks of $B$ consecutive hands, giving
the rolling statistics time to build a stable signal before the agent
tries to exploit it. After $H$ episodes, scheduling reverts to random.
$H$ and $B$ are HPO parameters.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Search}

We ran Optuna \cite{akiba2019optuna} with TPE sampling and MedianPruner
over a 15-dimensional space. Each trial trains for 300,000 timesteps
and is scored by a Rock-weighted objective:
\begin{equation}
\text{score} = 0.5 \cdot \text{BB}_{100}^{\text{Rock}}
             + 0.25 \cdot \text{BB}_{100}^{\text{Station}}
             + 0.25 \cdot \text{BB}_{100}^{\text{Maniac}}
\label{eq:hpo_obj}
\end{equation}

The Rock weight is doubled because farming the Maniac or CallingStation
is easy; good performance against the Rock is evidence that the agent
learned something real. The best configurations are in Table~\ref{tab:best_params}.

\subsection{Training and Evaluation}

Both models train for 1{,}000{,}000 timesteps using 4 parallel
environments (DummyVecEnv). A per-opponent callback evaluates BB/100
every 50{,}000 timesteps to track learning dynamics.

Performance is reported in \textit{big blinds per 100 hands} (BB/100):
\begin{equation}
\text{BB/100} = \frac{\text{total profit}}{\text{number of hands}} \times 100
\label{eq:bb100}
\end{equation}

where 1 BB = 4 chips. Final evaluation runs 10{,}000 hands against each
fixed archetype over 3 independent seeds. Model~B uses a 50-hand random
warm-up to populate the statistics buffer before evaluation starts.

\subsection{``The Tell'': Behavioral Matrix Analysis}

The most illuminating result is not the aggregate BB/100 but what
Model~B actually learned to do. Fig.~\ref{fig:behavioral} shows
Model~B's post-draw action distribution (Fold / Call / Raise) broken
down by opponent draw count (0, 1, or 3 cards) for each archetype.

\begin{figure*}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{behavioral_matrix.png}
  \caption{Model~B reads opponent draw counts as hand-strength signals:
    against the Rock, standing pat triggers cautious call/raise (50/43\%);
    drawing one card collapses both to 0\%, as that is an anomalous Rock
    action historically correlated with strong draws. Against the CallingStation,
    draw count barely changes anything---it is always safe to bet.}
  \label{fig:behavioral}
\end{figure*}

Against the CallingStation, the pattern is flat: Model~B calls or raises
regardless of draw count (0\% fold rate at draw 0 or draw 1). There is
nothing to read in a passive opponent's draws.

Against the Maniac, a single-card draw lifts Model~B's raise frequency
to 52\%. A loose opponent drawing one card is typically chasing a flush
or straight that usually misses, making it a good spot to apply pressure.

The sharpest pattern is against the Rock. When the Rock stands pat (draw
0), Model~B responds with 50\% call and 43\% raise. It treats this as a
strong-hand signal and stays in. When the Rock draws one card, Model~B's
call and raise both drop to 0\%. Drawing one is a rare Rock action that,
historically in training, correlated with high-value draws. The agent
learned to get out of the way. These are opponent-specific, draw-count-driven
heuristics, and Model~B discovered them without ever seeing an archetype
label.

Model~A variants serve as baselines: an \textit{Oracle} that receives
the correct archetype label at each switch, and a \textit{Blind} variant

\subsection{Statistical Validation}

We pooled 270{,}000 hands (3 opponents $\times$ 3 seeds $\times$ 10{,}000
hands). A paired $t$-test on per-hand profit gives $t = -2.183$,
$p = 0.029$: the Model~A advantage is significant at $\alpha = 0.05$.
Model~B vs.\ the random baseline is not close ($t = +106.85$,
$p \approx 0$). Model~B's 95\% confidence interval is $+1.71$ chips/hand
[1.65, 1.76], corresponding to $+170.87$ BB/100 [165.31, 176.44].

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{How Much Does the Label Actually Help?}

About 14 BB/100 on average, concentrated almost entirely in the Rock
matchup. The Rock emits sparse behavioral signals. It folds a lot, plays
few hands, and rarely deviates from its pattern. A rolling window of 50
hands captures some of this, but not as cleanly as a direct label. Against
the Maniac, the label advantage nearly disappears, because the Maniac's
draws and aggression are distinctive enough that 50 hands is plenty of
data.

This suggests a diminishing-returns effect: implicit modeling closes most
of the gap when the opponent is behaviorally distinctive, but struggles
against opponents whose signal is infrequent or weak.

\subsection{Why Model~B Beats the Oracle in Non-Stationary Play}

Model~B's rolling statistics give it a continuous, graded representation
of opponent behavior. When the opponent switches archetypes, the buffer
does not snap to the new label, it gradually shifts as new evidence
accumulates. This is actually useful during the transition window, when
the opponent's behavior is a mixture of old and new patterns. The Oracle
gets the correct label immediately, but the policy was trained on clean
archetypes, not mixtures; Model~B's gradual buffer update may better
match the policy's training distribution near switch points.

The behavioral matrix in Fig.~\ref{fig:behavioral} supports this: Model~B
is already conditioning on fine-grained draw-count statistics rather than
on a discrete type, so partial evidence is still usable.

\subsection{Limitations}

All three opponents are fixed-strategy scripts. The agent has never played
an adaptive opponent, a self-play adversary, or a human. Against someone
who notices they are being exploited and adjusts, Model~B's strategy would
need to update faster than the rolling window allows.

The limit betting structure removes stack-depth considerations that dominate
no-limit play. The cold-start problem---the $W=50$ window needs several
dozen hands before it stabilizes---also means Model~B is handicapped at
the start of a new session or right after an opponent switches.

Heads-up only. Multiplayer would require rethinking the observation design
substantially for optimal play.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We built a complete RL pipeline for 5-Card Draw Poker and used it to test
whether an agent can exploit behaviorally distinct opponents without knowing
which one it is facing. It can, at a cost of about 14 BB/100 versus an
agent that gets a direct label. The gap is real and statistically
significant, but modest enough to suggest that draw-count telemetry carries
most of the relevant information.

The behavioral matrix analysis is the clearest evidence of what the agent
actually learned. Model~B treats a Rock standing pat as a ``stay in'' signal
and a Rock drawing one card as a ``get out'' signal, with no explicit hand-range
model and no archetype identifier. That is the Tell. The agent discovered it
from reward alone.

In non-stationary play, the implicit agent outperforms an oracle-labeled
explicit agent, which is the more practically useful result: real opponents
do not come with labels, and they change.

Future directions include no-limit betting, self-play training, and combining
the rolling statistics with Bayesian hand-range inference to address the
Rock's sparse behavioral signal.
